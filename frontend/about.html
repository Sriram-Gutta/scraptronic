About Scraptronic

Why this site exists.

I'm Sriram Gutta, a computer engineering student at Purdue. I grew up in San Diego and I built Scraptronic because I had a drawer of dead phones, a stack of old laptop chargers, and a defunct external hard drive that I'd been telling myself I'd "figure out" for two years.

When I finally sat down to figure it out, the problem wasn't the recycling itself - San Diego actually has a lot of free e-waste drop-off options. The problem was that the information is scattered across half a dozen places: CalRecycle's database, the City of San Diego's hazardous waste page (which confusingly tells you they don't take e-waste), individual recycler websites, Earth911's locator, and a handful of nonprofits. No single map. No clear sense of which places take what. No indication that the aluminum in my old laptop is actually worth a few dollars.

Scraptronic is my attempt to bundle that scattered information into one place, focused on the San Diego area to start. It's also a working portfolio piece - a real platform built on real public data, with a live backend, no fake features, and source citations on every claim.

What it actually does

  • An interactive map of 18 hand-curated e-waste drop-off locations across San Diego County, each entry citing the public registry it came from.
  • A materials catalog with estimated regional scrap prices for the common stuff in your old electronics - aluminum, copper, circuit boards, lithium batteries, etc. - plus a small calculator that turns "5 lbs of copper" into a dollar estimate.
  • A personal recycling tracker backed entirely by your browser's localStorage. Log entries, watch the totals add up, progress through tiers, see your estimated CO₂ savings using EPA WARM model factors. No account, no signup, no data leaves your machine.
  • A small library of articles covering what counts as e-waste, how recycling actually works, why San Diego has the infrastructure it does, and how to prep your devices before drop-off.

Engineering notes

The frontend is plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript - no React, no build step, no framework. The map uses Leaflet with OpenStreetMap tiles. The backend is a small Python Flask API running on PythonAnywhere's free tier. User progress lives in localStorage, which means no auth, no database, and the architecture fits inside free hosting indefinitely.

Every page falls back to static JSON published with the frontend if the backend is unreachable, so the site keeps working even if PythonAnywhere is having a bad day. Source is on GitHub.

What's honest about this demo

Scraptronic shows you the estimated scrap value of materials based on publicly available regional prices - it does not pay you for recycling. The recycler does, when you bring them the materials. A production version of Scraptronic would partner with recyclers to intermediate payouts and take a small transaction fee; this version focuses on discovery, education, and progress tracking.

Recycler hours and phone numbers go stale. Call ahead. Scrap prices fluctuate weekly. Treat every number on this site as an estimate, not a quote.
